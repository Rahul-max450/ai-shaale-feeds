Chapter Overview: On Children by Kahlil Gibran

English Overview: "On Children" is a profound poetic essay by Kahlil Gibran that redefines the parent-child relationship. Gibran argues that children are not the possessions of their parents but are independent beings who come through them as life's longing for itself. The poem emphasizes that parents should provide love but not impose their own thoughts, as children have their own unique destinies. Using the metaphor of the archer, the bow, and the arrow, Gibran illustrates that parents are merely the instruments (bows) through which God (the Archer) sends children (arrows) into the future.

Kannada Translation: ಖಲೀಲ್ ಜಿಬ್ರಾನ್ ಅವರ "ಆನ್ ಚಿಲ್ಡ್ರನ್" (ಮಕ್ಕಳ ಕುರಿತು) ಒಂದು ಆಳವಾದ ಕವಿತೆಯಾಗಿದೆ. ಇದು ಪೋಷಕರು ಮತ್ತು ಮಕ್ಕಳ ನಡುವಿನ ಸಂಬಂಧವನ್ನು ಮರು ವ್ಯಾಖ್ಯಾನಿಸುತ್ತದೆ. ಮಕ್ಕಳು ಪೋಷಕರ ಆಸ್ತಿಯಲ್ಲ, ಅವರು ಸ್ವತಂತ್ರ ಜೀವಿಗಳು ಎಂದು ಜಿಬ್ರಾನ್ ಪ್ರತಿಪಾದಿಸುತ್ತಾರೆ. ಪೋಷಕರು ತಮ್ಮ ಮಕ್ಕಳಿಗೆ ಪ್ರೀತಿಯನ್ನು ನೀಡಬೇಕು, ಆದರೆ ತಮ್ಮ ಸ್ವಂತ ಆಲೋಚನೆಗಳನ್ನು ಅವರ ಮೇಲೆ ಹೇರಬಾರದು, ಏಕೆಂದರೆ ಮಕ್ಕಳು ತಮ್ಮದೇ ಆದ ಭವಿಷ್ಯವನ್ನು ಹೊಂದಿರುತ್ತಾರೆ ಎಂದು ಕವಿತೆ ಒತ್ತಿಹೇಳುತ್ತದೆ. ಬಿಲ್ಲುಗಾರ, ಬಿಲ್ಲು ಮತ್ತು ಬಾಣದ ರೂಪಕವನ್ನು ಬಳಸುವ ಮೂಲಕ, ಪೋಷಕರು ಕೇವಲ ಸಾಧನಗಳಾಗಿದ್ದು, ಅವರ ಮೂಲಕ ದೇವರು (ಬಿಲ್ಲುಗಾರ) ಮಕ್ಕಳನ್ನು (ಬಾಣಗಳನ್ನು) ಭವಿಷ್ಯದತ್ತ ಕಳುಹಿಸುತ್ತಾನೆ ಎಂದು ಜಿಬ್ರಾನ್ ವಿವರಿಸುತ್ತಾರೆ.

Poem Memory Support

1. Poem Summary: The poem addresses the nature of parenthood. Gibran suggests that children are independent souls who belong to the future. Parents are the vessels that bring them into the world, but they do not own them. Parents should guide them but allow them to follow their own paths.

2. Central Idea: The central idea is the concept of detachment in love. Parents must love their children without being possessive, recognizing that children are individuals with their own thoughts and destinies.

3. Theme: The themes include individuality, the passage of time, the role of parents as facilitators, and the spiritual connection between God, parents, and children.

4. Poet Message: Gibran urges parents to be "stable bows" that support their children, while accepting that the children must fly forward toward their own goals, independent of the parents' past or personal desires.

5. Easy Memorization Notes: Remember the key metaphors: Children = Arrows; Parents = Bows; God = The Archer. Remember the phrase "Life's longing for itself" to describe why children are born.

6. 5 Mark Answer (The Metaphor of Bow and Arrow): The metaphor of the bow and arrow is central to the poem. The poet describes parents as the "bows" and children as the "living arrows." The Archer (God) uses the bow to launch the arrow. This signifies that parents are merely the instruments through which children are sent into the world. Just as an archer needs a stable bow to shoot an arrow far, children need stable, supportive parents to succeed. However, the arrow must eventually leave the bow to reach its target. This teaches parents to be supportive but not controlling, allowing children to pursue their own paths in life.

7. 10 Mark Answer (Critique of Parental Expectations): Kahlil Gibran’s "On Children" offers a revolutionary perspective on parenting. He begins by stating, "Your children are not your children," challenging the traditional notion of ownership. He argues that children are "sons and daughters of Life's longing for itself." Parents provide the physical body, but the soul belongs to the future. Gibran advises parents to give love, but not their own thoughts, as children have their own minds. He warns against trying to make children like oneself, because life does not move backward. The most powerful imagery is that of the Archer. God is the Archer who bends the parents (the bows) to launch the children (the arrows) toward the infinite. This implies that parents have a responsibility to be strong and stable, but they must also be willing to let go. Ultimately, the poem encourages parents to find joy in their role as guides rather than possessors, fostering a relationship based on respect, freedom, and spiritual trust.
