Chapter 12: The Voter by Chinua Achebe

English Overview

The story "The Voter" by Chinua Achebe is a satirical look at the electoral process in a fictional Nigerian village called Umuofia. It centers on Rufus Okeke, known as 'Roof', a popular and influential young man who manages the election campaign for the incumbent, Chief Marcus Ibe. The story highlights the corruption, manipulation, and the commodification of votes in a democratic system. Roof, who is an expert in campaigning, finds himself in a moral dilemma when he is bribed by the opposition party to vote for their candidate, Maduka, under the oath of a local deity, 'iyi'. The story culminates on election day, where Roof, torn between his loyalty to Marcus and his fear of the deity, tears his ballot paper in two and casts half for each candidate, symbolizing the complexity and moral compromise inherent in the political process.

Comprehension II

Question 1: Trace the change in the attitude of the villagers before the second election. Give reasons.

Answer: Initially, the villagers were blindly loyal to Marcus Ibe. However, over five years, they realized that politics brought wealth only to Marcus, while they remained in poverty. They saw his new cars and mansion, and felt that they had been taken for granted. They decided that they would not give their votes for free this time, as they had done five years ago. They expected tangible benefits for their support, showing a shift from blind loyalty to a transactional approach.

Question 2: What was the justification for the formation of the POP?

Answer: The Progressive Organization Party (POP) was formed by tribes down the coast. Their stated justification was to save themselves from "total political, cultural, social and religious annihilation." They aimed to challenge the dominance of the People's Alliance Party (PAP) and provide an alternative, though they were essentially using money to buy votes.

Question 3: 'Roof is an intelligent manipulator'. Justify with reference to the story.

Answer: Roof is a master of political maneuvering. He understands the pulse of the electorate perfectly. He knows how to use the villagers' emotions, their respect for tradition, and their greed to secure votes for Marcus. He organizes secret campaigns, manages the elders, and even handles the opposition's bribe with cunning. His ability to switch between English and Ibo, and his quick thinking on election day, prove that he is an intelligent, albeit morally flexible, manipulator who treats politics as a game of influence.

Comprehension III

Question 1: The POP campaign leader's meeting with Roof shows the misuse of transparency in a democratic set up. Discuss.

Answer: The meeting between the POP leader and Roof highlights how democratic processes are subverted by corruption. Instead of a transparent debate on policies, the meeting is a clandestine transaction where money is used to buy a vote. The use of the 'iyi' (a deity) to bind Roof to his promise adds a layer of superstitious coercion. This shows that in a system where voters are poor and uneducated, transparency is replaced by bribery and intimidation, turning the democratic right to vote into a commodity to be sold to the highest bidder.

Question 2: 'To every human comes a time of reckoning'. How does Roof's dilemma on the day of election reflect this?

Answer: Roof's moment of reckoning arrives when he stands in the polling booth. He has taken money from the opposition and sworn on the 'iyi' to vote for Maduka, but his loyalty and professional commitment are to Marcus. He realizes that he cannot satisfy both. His internal conflict represents the moral crisis of a man who has played with the democratic process for personal gain. By tearing the ballot paper, he attempts to resolve this crisis, but it ultimately shows that his integrity has been compromised by the very system he helped manipulate.

Question 3: What comment does the story offer on electoral system? Is it relevant?

Answer: The story offers a scathing critique of an electoral system where money and influence override the true will of the people. It portrays voters as victims of their own poverty and politicians as opportunists who view elections as a means to personal wealth. This is highly relevant in many modern democracies where electioneering has become an expensive, corrupt, and manipulative exercise. The story serves as a reminder that democracy is fragile and can easily be corrupted if the electorate is not empowered and informed.

Question 4: 'Democracy is more than holding elections regularly.' Do you think the story highlights this statement?

Answer: Yes, the story perfectly illustrates that elections alone do not constitute a healthy democracy. While Umuofia holds elections, the process is devoid of democratic values like informed choice, equality, and accountability. The villagers are manipulated, their votes are bought, and the leaders are self-serving. True democracy requires an educated and vigilant citizenry that votes based on merit and policy, not on bribes or tribal loyalties. The story highlights that without these values, regular elections are merely a facade for power-grabbing.

Section 4: Extra Exam Questions

Question 1: Describe the character of Roof and his role in the election campaign.

Answer: Roof is the central character of the story, a young man who is highly popular in his village of Umuofia. He is energetic, resourceful, and possesses a deep understanding of the local political landscape. Having spent time in the city, he is more worldly-wise than the elders, which makes him an effective campaign manager for Chief Marcus Ibe. Roof is not just a supporter; he is a strategist who knows how to manipulate the villagers' sentiments. He organizes secret campaigns, manages the distribution of money, and uses his influence to ensure that the villagers remain loyal to the People's Alliance Party. His character represents the modern, opportunistic youth who find power and profit in the corrupt political machinery of the time.

Question 2: How does the author use the 'iyi' to build tension in the story?

Answer: The 'iyi' serves as a powerful tool of psychological and spiritual coercion in the story. When the POP campaign leader brings the 'iyi' to Roof, it transforms a simple political bribe into a matter of life and death. The 'iyi' is a native deity, and the villagers, including Roof, hold a deep, superstitious fear of it. By forcing Roof to swear on the 'iyi' that he will vote for Maduka, the opposition party effectively traps him. This creates immense tension because Roof is now caught between his worldly loyalty to Marcus and his spiritual fear of the deity. This tension reaches its peak on election day, driving the climax of the story where Roof is forced to make a desperate, split-second decision to save himself from the wrath of the deity.

Question 3: Discuss the significance of the title 'The Voter'.

Answer: The title 'The Voter' is deeply ironic. While it refers to Roof as a voter, it also encompasses the entire electorate of Umuofia. In a true democracy, a voter is an empowered individual who makes a free and informed choice. However, in the story, the 'voter' is a victim of manipulation, poverty, and corruption. Roof, despite his intelligence, is reduced to a pawn who sells his vote for five pounds. The title highlights the degradation of the democratic process, where the act of voting loses its sanctity and becomes a mere transaction. It forces the reader to question the meaning of the vote and the responsibility of the voter in a system that is designed to exploit them rather than represent them.
