Class: 10th English • CHAPTER 06 - JAZZ POEM TWO

SECTION 1: CHAPTER OVERVIEW

English Overview

The poem "Jazz Poem Two" by Carl Wendall Hines, Jr. portrays an aging, weary jazz musician who appears physically broken and neglected. Despite his ragged appearance—wearing a faded shirt, an undone necktie, and shoes stuffed with paper—he undergoes a profound transformation the moment he begins to play his alto saxophone. The instrument, held by a wire coat hanger, becomes his voice. Through his music, he transcends his identity as a struggling "Black Man" and becomes a "Bird," soaring high through his art. The poem explores themes of artistic passion, the healing power of music, and the dignity found in creative expression.

SECTION 2: POEM MEMORY SUPPORT

1. Poem Summary

The poem describes an old, weary jazz musician standing in solitude. He looks pathetic with his wrinkled face, closed eyes, and shabby clothes. However, when he lifts his saxophone, he transforms. He preaches the "Black Gospel of Jazz" through his music. He stops being a mere man and becomes a "Bird" that flies higher and higher, transcending his worldly suffering through the power of his art.

2. Central Idea

The central idea is the transformative power of art. Music allows an individual to rise above their physical suffering, poverty, and social identity to reach a state of spiritual freedom and transcendence.

3. Theme

The themes include the dignity of the artist, the contrast between physical decay and spiritual vitality, and the use of music as a medium for self-expression and liberation.

4. Poet Message

The poet conveys that true artists possess a divine gift. Even when life is harsh and one is physically worn out, the passion for one's craft can provide a sense of purpose and a way to "fly" above the difficulties of existence.

5. Easy Memorization Notes

  • Stanza 1: Physical description (wrinkled face, closed eyes, shabby clothes).
  • Stanza 2: The musician's solitude and his focus on the bass line.
  • Stanza 3: The transformation (the saxophone, the "Black Gospel of Jazz").
  • Stanza 4: The climax (becoming a "Bird" and flying high).

6. 5 Mark Answer: Describe the transformation of the Jazz player.

In the beginning, the jazz player is described as a pathetic figure. He is old, his face is wrinkled with the "wearies of living," and he wears shabby clothes with paper in his shoes. He stands in self-brought solitude with his eyes closed. However, the moment he lifts his alto saxophone to his lips, a magical transformation occurs. He is no longer just a man or a "Black Man" struggling with life; he becomes a "Bird." Through his screaming notes and chords, he finds a spiritual release. He gathers his wings and flies high, transcending his physical reality. This transformation shows that music is not just a profession for him, but a medium through which he escapes his pain and finds his true, liberated self.

7. 10 Mark Answer: How does the poet use the image of the 'Ancient Mariner' and the 'Bird' to portray the musician?

The poet uses the reference to the 'Ancient Mariner' to establish the musician's weary, burdened, and isolated state. Just as the Mariner in Coleridge’s poem is a man haunted by his past and suffering from a curse, the jazz musician is haunted by the "wearies of living." His wrinkled face and closed eyes suggest a man who has seen too much and is burdened by the weight of his existence. He stands in "self-brought solitude," disconnected from the world around him, much like the Mariner on his lonely ship.

However, the poet contrasts this heavy, grounded image with the metaphor of the "Bird." When the musician plays, he sheds his earthly burdens. The "Bird" represents freedom, grace, and the ability to rise above the mundane. The lines "flies high / high / higher" visually represent this ascent. The music acts as the wings that allow him to escape his physical decay. By the end of the poem, the musician has successfully transcended his identity as a "Black Man" and entered a realm of pure artistic expression. The poet effectively uses these two contrasting images to show that while the body may grow old and tired, the human spirit, through art, can achieve a state of flight and absolute freedom.
