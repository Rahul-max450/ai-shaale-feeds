Class: 10th English • CHAPTER 02 - GRANDMA CLIMBS A TREE

SECTION 1: CHAPTER OVERVIEW

English Overview

The poem "Grandma Climbs a Tree" by Ruskin Bond is a delightful narrative about a grandmother who possesses a unique passion for climbing trees, even at the age of sixty-two. Despite societal expectations that she should grow old "gracefully," she chooses to "grow disgracefully" by continuing her adventurous hobby. The poem highlights the bond between the grandmother, her son, and her grandchild, showcasing their support for her unconventional desires, which culminates in the construction of a treehouse for her.

SECTION 2: POEM MEMORY SUPPORT

1. Poem Summary

The poem describes a grandmother who is a "genius" at climbing trees. Even at sixty-two, she maintains this childhood skill. When her family advises her to grow old gracefully, she humorously retorts that she will grow "disgracefully." After a minor accident where she gets stuck in a tree, the doctor orders bed rest. Finding this confinement unbearable, she demands a treehouse. Her son, respecting her wishes, builds her a beautiful treehouse where she lives happily, enjoying her sherry and her independence.

2. Central Idea

The central idea is that age is merely a number and should not restrict one's spirit or passions. It celebrates individuality and the importance of family support in honoring the unique desires of loved ones.

3. Theme

The themes include aging with spirit, the breaking of social stereotypes, family love, and the freedom to pursue one's happiness regardless of age.

4. Poet Message

Ruskin Bond conveys that life should be lived on one's own terms. One should not let societal pressure dictate how to behave or age, as long as it brings joy and does not harm others.

5. Easy Memorization Notes

  • Stanza 1: Introduction to Grandma as a genius tree climber.
  • Stanza 2: Her defiance of "growing old gracefully."
  • Stanza 3: The accident and the doctor's order for bed rest.
  • Stanza 4: Her demand for a treehouse and the son's compliance.
  • Stanza 5: The happy conclusion of her living in the treehouse.

6. 5 Mark Answer

The poem "Grandma Climbs a Tree" portrays the grandmother as a vibrant and unconventional character. Unlike most people her age, she finds joy in climbing trees, a skill she learned at age six. When society pressures her to act her age, she boldly declares she will grow "disgracefully." This shows her strong personality and refusal to conform to rigid social norms. Her family, instead of being embarrassed, supports her. The son builds her a treehouse, and the grandson brings her drinks. This poem teaches us that true happiness comes from being authentic and that family support is crucial in allowing individuals to maintain their identity, regardless of their age.

7. 10 Mark Answer

Ruskin Bond’s "Grandma Climbs a Tree" is a heartwarming tribute to a grandmother who defies the conventional definition of aging. The poem begins by establishing her as a "genius" because of her extraordinary ability to climb trees, a hobby she maintained even at sixty-two. The poet contrasts the societal expectation of "growing old gracefully" with the grandmother’s witty retort of "growing disgracefully," highlighting her rebellious and joyful spirit. The narrative takes a turn when she gets stuck in a tree, leading to a doctor’s order for bed rest. This period of confinement is described as a "brief season in hell," emphasizing how vital her freedom and connection to nature are to her well-being. The climax of the poem is the construction of a treehouse. Her son, recognizing his duty, builds her a home in the branches, which allows her to live in state. The poem concludes with a beautiful image of the grandmother and her grandchild sharing a drink in the treehouse, symbolizing the triumph of her spirit. Through this, Bond emphasizes that age should not be a barrier to passion. The poem is a celebration of individuality, the importance of nature, and the unconditional love of a family that respects the unique choices of its members. It serves as a reminder that life is meant to be lived with enthusiasm, and one should never lose the "child" within, no matter how many years pass.

SECTION 4: EXTRA EXAM QUESTIONS

Question 1: How does the poet portray the grandmother's personality in the poem? (5 Marks)

Answer: The poet portrays the grandmother as a spirited, unconventional, and independent woman. She is described as a "genius" not because of academic achievements, but because of her unique talent for climbing trees. She refuses to conform to the societal expectation of "growing old gracefully," choosing instead to live life on her own terms. Her wit is evident in her reply to those who criticize her, and her determination is shown when she demands a treehouse after being confined to bed. She is a character who values freedom and nature over comfort, making her a memorable and inspiring figure in the poem.

Question 2: Discuss the role of the father in the poem. (5 Marks)

Answer: The father in the poem is a model of a supportive and understanding son. When the grandmother expresses her desire to live in a treehouse, he does not dismiss her as being childish or unreasonable. Instead, he acknowledges his duty to make her happy. He immediately agrees to her request and works diligently to build a treehouse with windows and a door. His actions demonstrate that he respects his mother's individuality and choices. By facilitating her wish, he ensures that she can continue to live with dignity and joy, proving that he is a caring and considerate son who prioritizes his mother's happiness above social norms.

Question 3: Why did the grandmother find the week in bed like a "brief season in hell"? (5 Marks)

Answer: The grandmother was a woman who loved the outdoors, nature, and the freedom of climbing trees. Being confined to her bedroom for a week, as recommended by the doctor, was a complete contrast to her active lifestyle. The breeze whispering of summer and the dancing leaves outside served as constant reminders of what she was missing. For someone who was "happier in a tree than in a lift," being restricted to a bed felt like a punishment. This emotional and physical restriction made the week feel like a "brief season in hell," highlighting her deep-seated need for movement, nature, and independence.
