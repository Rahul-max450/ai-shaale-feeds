SECTION 1: CHAPTER OVERVIEW

English Overview

The poem "The Blind Boy" by Colley Cibber is a poignant exploration of the perspective of a visually impaired child. The boy expresses his curiosity about the nature of light and sight, which he has never experienced. Despite his disability, he displays remarkable resilience and contentment. He explains that he creates his own "day" and "night" through his sleep and play. He urges those who pity him to stop, as he does not miss what he has never known. The poem concludes with a powerful assertion of self-worth, where the boy declares himself a "king" in his own mind, choosing happiness over sorrow.

SECTION 2: POEM MEMORY SUPPORT

1. Poem Summary

The poem is a dialogue between a blind boy and the sighted world. The boy asks what "light" is, as he cannot see it. He hears people talk about the sun's brightness and warmth; he can feel the warmth but cannot comprehend the visual aspect of day or night. He finds his own rhythm of life through sleep and play. He asks people not to pity him for his blindness, as he is content with his life. He concludes that he is as happy as a king because he refuses to let his lack of sight destroy his peace of mind.

2. Central Idea

The central idea is that happiness is a state of mind rather than a result of physical perfection. The blind boy teaches us that contentment comes from within and that one should not mourn what one does not possess.

3. Theme

The themes are resilience, contentment, the power of the human spirit, and the subjectivity of happiness.

4. Poet Message

Colley Cibber conveys that we should not pity those with disabilities, as they often possess a strength of character that allows them to find joy in their own unique way. We should focus on what we have rather than what we lack.

5. Easy Memorization Notes

  • Stanza 1: Questions about light and the blessings of sight.
  • Stanza 2: The contrast between feeling the sun's warmth and the concept of day/night.
  • Stanza 3: Creating his own day/night through sleep and play.
  • Stanza 4: Requesting people to stop mourning his "woe."
  • Stanza 5: The final declaration of being a "king" despite his blindness.

6. 5 Mark Answer (The Blind Boy's Attitude)

The blind boy in the poem exhibits an extraordinary attitude towards life. Despite being deprived of the gift of sight, he does not wallow in self-pity. He is curious about the world but remains grounded in his own experiences. He feels the warmth of the sun, which is enough for him to understand its presence. He creates his own world of day and night through his activities like sleeping and playing. He finds the pity of others unnecessary because he does not feel the loss of something he has never known. His ability to maintain a cheerful mind proves that happiness is internal. By calling himself a "king," he asserts his dignity and proves that his spirit is not broken by his physical limitation.

7. 10 Mark Answer (Critical Analysis of the Poem)

Colley Cibber’s "The Blind Boy" is a profound reflection on the nature of happiness and the human condition. The poem is structured as a series of questions and reflections from a blind child to the sighted world. The boy’s inquiry about "light" highlights the gap between the sensory experience of the sighted and the imaginative world of the blind. The poet uses the boy’s voice to challenge the societal tendency to pity the disabled. The boy’s assertion, "My day or night myself I make," is a powerful metaphor for self-reliance. He does not allow his physical condition to dictate his emotional state. The poem suggests that "woe" is often a projection of the observer rather than the reality of the person being observed. The boy’s refusal to let his lack of sight destroy his "cheer of mind" serves as a lesson for all readers. He concludes that he is a "king" because he has mastered his own emotions. This poem remains a timeless reminder that true freedom and joy are found in the mind, regardless of physical circumstances. It encourages us to cultivate patience and contentment, ensuring that we do not let our perceived lacks define our worth.

Question 1: Why does the blind boy call himself a king? (5 Marks)

English Answer: The blind boy calls himself a king because he has achieved mastery over his own mind. Even though he is physically blind and lacks the "blessings of sight," he does not allow this limitation to dictate his happiness. A king is someone who is in control and is not easily defeated by circumstances. By refusing to let his lack of sight destroy his "cheer of mind," he demonstrates a superior strength of character. He finds contentment within himself, which is a form of internal sovereignty. Thus, he feels as powerful and satisfied as a king, proving that true happiness is independent of external conditions.

Question 2: How does the poet use the blind boy to criticize the pity of sighted people? (5 Marks)

English Answer: The poet uses the blind boy to highlight the irony of pity. Sighted people often feel sorry for the blind, assuming that their life must be miserable. However, the boy points out that he cannot miss what he has never known. He asks, "What I ne’er can know," implying that his lack of sight is not a "woe" to him, but rather a neutral state of being. By asking people to stop mourning his condition, he shifts the perspective from the observer to the experienced. He shows that the pity of others is unnecessary and perhaps even intrusive. The boy’s resilience makes the sighted people's sympathy seem misplaced, as he is perfectly capable of finding joy in his own way.

Question 3: Explain the significance of the lines "My day or night myself I make" in the context of the poem. (5 Marks)

English Answer: These lines are central to the poem's theme of self-reliance and internal happiness. For a sighted person, day and night are defined by the presence or absence of sunlight. However, for the blind boy, these concepts are abstract. He redefines "day" and "night" based on his own activities—sleeping and playing. This shows that he is not a passive victim of his blindness but an active creator of his own reality. He does not wait for the world to provide him with light; he generates his own sense of time and purpose. This line emphasizes that our perception of the world is shaped by our actions and mindset, not just by our physical senses. It is a powerful statement of independence and psychological freedom.
