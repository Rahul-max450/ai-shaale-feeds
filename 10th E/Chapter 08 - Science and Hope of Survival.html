SECTION 1 : CHAPTER OVERVIEW

English Overview

The chapter "Science and Hope of Survival" by Keilis-Borok explores the life and perspective of a scientist. It highlights that science is not just a profession but an exciting adventure driven by curiosity and the desire to uncover nature's mysteries. The author recounts his experience during the Cold War, where he was summoned to Geneva to help solve the problem of detecting secret underground nuclear explosions. This event demonstrates how theoretical scientific knowledge can have direct, life-saving applications. The chapter emphasizes that science, when used humanely, is an indispensable guardian of civilization, capable of addressing global challenges like disasters and economic crises.

Question: “A paradox may refer to a person, a thing or a situation that has two opposite features and therefore seems strange.” In the light of this definition, how is ‘MAD’ a paradox? How could MAD affect the super power nations?

English Answer (5 Marks): 'MAD' (Mutually Assured Destruction) is a classic paradox because it uses the threat of total annihilation to maintain peace. It is strange because the very weapons designed to destroy the enemy are the ones preventing the war from starting. The paradox lies in the fact that the superpowers were "protecting" the world by holding the power to destroy it completely. This affected the nations by creating a stalemate; neither side could attack without ensuring their own destruction. This "thin thread" of fear forced the superpowers to communicate and eventually seek agreements, as the cost of war became too high for anyone to survive. It turned the global standoff into a delicate balance where survival depended on not using the weapons they had spent trillions to build.

Question 2: Explain the role of scientists in the Nuclear Test Ban agreement.

English Answer: Scientists played a crucial role in the Nuclear Test Ban agreement by bridging the gap between opposing superpowers. During the Cold War, political tensions made communication difficult. However, scientists from both sides of the "Iron Curtain" shared a common language based on hard evidence, expertise, and self-criticism. They were able to solve the technical problem of distinguishing between natural earthquakes and secret underground nuclear explosions. By providing a clear, scientific solution to the detection problem, they removed the technical barriers that prevented politicians from reaching an agreement. Their ability to work together despite cultural and political differences ultimately paved the way for one of the most important peace decisions of that era.

Question 3: How does basic research contribute to the survival of our civilization?

English Answer: Basic research is the foundation for our survival because it provides the tools and knowledge to face both natural and man-made disasters. Whether it is predicting earthquakes, finding cures for diseases like cancer, or developing new sources of energy, basic research creates the "springboard" for innovation. As our world faces threats like environmental catastrophes, economic crises, and terrorism, we cannot rely on money alone to solve these issues. We need intellectual resources and scientific breakthroughs to develop disaster preparedness industries. By investing in basic research, we ensure that we have the technology and understanding necessary to protect our civilization and move safely into the future.
