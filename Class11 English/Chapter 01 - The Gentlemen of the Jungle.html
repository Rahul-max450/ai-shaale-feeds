Chapter 1: The Gentlemen of the Jungle

SECTION 1: CHAPTER OVERVIEW

English Overview

"The Gentlemen of the Jungle" is a political fable by Jomo Kenyatta, the first president of Kenya. The story serves as an allegory for the colonization of Africa by European powers. It depicts a man who allows an elephant to shelter its trunk in his hut during a storm, only for the elephant to take over the entire hut. The subsequent "Commission of Enquiry," composed entirely of animals, consistently rules in favor of the animals, stripping the man of his property. The story concludes with the man outsmarting the animals by building a large hut that lures them all inside, which he then burns down, reclaiming his independence. It is a powerful satire on the injustice of colonial rule and the futility of seeking justice from those who benefit from oppression.

Kannada Translation (ಕನ್ನಡ ಅನುವಾದ)

"ದಿ ಜೆಂಟಲ್‌ಮೆನ್ ಆಫ್ ದಿ ಜಂಗಲ್" (ಕಾಡಿನ ಸಭ್ಯರು) ಕಥೆಯು ಕೀನ್ಯಾದ ಮೊದಲ ಅಧ್ಯಕ್ಷರಾದ ಜೋಮೊ ಕೆನ್ಯಾಟ್ಟಾ ಅವರು ಬರೆದ ರಾಜಕೀಯ ನೀತಿಕಥೆಯಾಗಿದೆ. ಈ ಕಥೆಯು ಆಫ್ರಿಕಾದಲ್ಲಿ ಯುರೋಪಿಯನ್ ವಸಾಹತುಶಾಹಿಯ ವಿರುದ್ಧದ ಒಂದು ರೂಪಕವಾಗಿದೆ. ಮಳೆಯ ಸಮಯದಲ್ಲಿ ಆನೆಯೊಂದು ತನ್ನ ಸೊಂಡಲನ್ನು ಮಾತ್ರ ಗುಡಿಸಲಿನೊಳಗೆ ಇಡಲು ಅನುಮತಿ ಕೇಳುತ್ತದೆ, ಆದರೆ ನಂತರ ಅದು ಇಡೀ ಗುಡಿಸಲನ್ನೇ ಆಕ್ರಮಿಸಿಕೊಳ್ಳುತ್ತದೆ. ನಂತರ ರಚನೆಯಾದ 'ತನಿಖಾ ಆಯೋಗ'ವು ಸಂಪೂರ್ಣವಾಗಿ ಪ್ರಾಣಿಗಳಿಂದಲೇ ಕೂಡಿದ್ದು, ಅದು ಯಾವಾಗಲೂ ಪ್ರಾಣಿಗಳ ಪರವಾಗಿಯೇ ತೀರ್ಪು ನೀಡುತ್ತದೆ. ಕೊನೆಯಲ್ಲಿ, ಮನುಷ್ಯನು ಪ್ರಾಣಿಗಳನ್ನು ಬುದ್ಧಿವಂತಿಕೆಯಿಂದ ಸೋಲಿಸುತ್ತಾನೆ; ಅವನು ಒಂದು ದೊಡ್ಡ ಗುಡಿಸಲನ್ನು ನಿರ್ಮಿಸಿ, ಎಲ್ಲಾ ಪ್ರಾಣಿಗಳನ್ನು ಅದರೊಳಗೆ ಬರುವಂತೆ ಮಾಡಿ, ನಂತರ ಆ ಗುಡಿಸಲಿಗೆ ಬೆಂಕಿ ಹಚ್ಚಿ ತನ್ನ ಸ್ವಾತಂತ್ರ್ಯವನ್ನು ಮರಳಿ ಪಡೆಯುತ್ತಾನೆ. ಇದು ವಸಾಹತುಶಾಹಿ ಆಡಳಿತದ ಅನ್ಯಾಯ ಮತ್ತು ದಬ್ಬಾಳಿಕೆ ನಡೆಸುವವರಿಂದಲೇ ನ್ಯಾಯವನ್ನು ನಿರೀಕ್ಷಿಸುವುದರ ವ್ಯರ್ಥತೆಯನ್ನು ಎತ್ತಿ ತೋರಿಸುವ ಒಂದು ವ್ಯಂಗ್ಯಚಿತ್ರಣವಾಗಿದೆ.

SECTION 4: EXTRA EXAM QUESTIONS

Question 1: Discuss the role of the Commission of Enquiry in the story. How does it represent the colonial administration?

Answer: The Commission of Enquiry is a central element of the satire in the story. It is composed of the elephant's friends, such as the Rhinoceros, Buffalo, and Leopard, with the Fox as chairman. This composition ensures that the Commission is inherently biased. It represents the colonial administration, which often set up committees or commissions to resolve disputes between colonizers and the colonized. These commissions were designed to give a veneer of legality and fairness to the theft of land, while in reality, they were tools used to legitimize the oppression of the native people. By excluding the man, the Commission highlights how colonial powers denied the colonized a voice in their own governance.

Question 2: Analyze the character of the man in the story. How does his attitude change from the beginning to the end?

Answer: At the beginning of the story, the man is portrayed as kind, naive, and trusting. He believes in the goodness of others and respects the "jungle law," hoping for a fair resolution. However, as the story progresses and he faces repeated betrayals by the animals and the biased Commissions, his attitude shifts from passivity to realization. He learns that kindness is often exploited by the powerful. By the end, he becomes pragmatic and strategic. He realizes that "peace is costly" and that he must take matters into his own hands to secure his freedom. His final act of burning the hut shows his transition from a victim to a revolutionary who understands the necessity of force against systemic injustice.

Question 3: Explain the significance of the proverb "Ng-enda thi ndeagaga motegi" used by the man.

Answer: The proverb "Ng-enda thi ndeagaga motegi," which means "there is nothing that treads on the earth that cannot be trapped," is the turning point of the story. It signifies the man's realization that the oppressors, no matter how powerful they appear, have weaknesses and can be outsmarted. It reflects the idea that while one can be fooled or oppressed for a period, the human spirit and the desire for justice will eventually find a way to overcome. This proverb serves as a warning to the oppressors that their dominance is not eternal and that their arrogance will eventually lead to their downfall. It marks the man's shift from a state of helplessness to a state of empowered resistance.
