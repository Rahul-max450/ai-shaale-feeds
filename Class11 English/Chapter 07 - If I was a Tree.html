Chapter 7: If I Was a Tree - Mudnakudu Chinnaswamy

Section 1: Chapter Overview

English Overview: "If I was a Tree" is a poignant poem by Mudnakudu Chinnaswamy that critiques the rigid and discriminatory caste system prevalent in society. The poet uses the metaphor of a tree to highlight the hypocrisy of human social structures. Unlike humans, nature—represented by birds, sunlight, breeze, and rain—does not discriminate based on caste or social status. The poem contrasts the purity of nature with the "defilement" perceived by humans, ultimately questioning the validity of such social barriers.

Section 2: Poem Memory Support

1. Poem Summary: The poet expresses a desire to be a tree to escape the humiliation of the caste system. He observes that birds build nests without asking his caste, and sunlight touches him without feeling "defiled." He finds companionship with nature, which is free from human prejudice. Even the sacred cow, which carries gods, interacts with him naturally. Finally, he reflects that even in death, as firewood or a bier, he might finally be considered "pure" or useful, highlighting the irony that he is only accepted when he is no longer a living human being.

2. Central Idea: The central idea is the protest against social discrimination and the caste system. It highlights how nature is inclusive and egalitarian, whereas human society is exclusionary and judgmental.

3. Theme: The themes are social inequality, the hypocrisy of caste-based purity/pollution, the healing power of nature, and the longing for human dignity.

4. Poet Message: The poet conveys that human-made barriers like caste are artificial and cruel. He urges society to learn from nature, which treats all beings with equality and without prejudice.

5. Easy Memorization Notes: Remember the sequence: Bird (nest), Sunlight (shadow), Breeze/Leaves (friendship), Rain (dog-eater), Mother Earth (fleeing), Sacred Cow (gods), and finally Death (firewood/bier).

6. 5 Mark Answer: The poem "If I was a Tree" serves as a powerful critique of the caste system. The poet feels that human society is burdened by the concept of "defilement." By wishing to be a tree, he highlights that nature is free from such prejudices. A bird does not ask for caste before building a nest; sunlight does not feel defiled by his shadow. These examples show that nature is inherently egalitarian. The poet contrasts this with the human world, where he is treated as an outcast. The poem effectively uses the tree as a symbol of dignity and equality, suggesting that the natural world is far more civilized than the human world, which is trapped in the narrow-mindedness of caste and social hierarchy.

7. 10 Mark Answer: Mudnakudu Chinnaswamy’s poem "If I was a Tree" is a profound reflection on the dehumanizing effects of the caste system. The poet uses the tree as a metaphor to contrast the purity of nature with the impurity of human social practices. In the human world, the poet is subjected to the stigma of being "untouchable," where his shadow is considered polluting. However, in the realm of nature, he finds acceptance. The bird builds its nest without inquiry, the sunlight embraces him, and the breeze offers friendship. The poet also touches upon the irony of religious hypocrisy; the "sacred" cow, which carries three hundred thousand gods, touches the tree without hesitation, yet humans maintain distance. The most biting irony is reserved for the end: the poet realizes that he might only be accepted or "purified" after death, when his body is turned into firewood or a bier. This highlights the tragedy that in a caste-ridden society, a human being is denied dignity in life and is only "purified" through death. The poem is a scathing satire on the absurdity of social discrimination, urging readers to look beyond artificial labels and embrace the equality that nature inherently practices.

Section 4: Extra Exam Questions

1. How does the poet use the 'sacred cow' to criticize religious hypocrisy?
The poet mentions that the sacred cow, which is worshipped by society and is believed to shelter three hundred thousand gods, does not discriminate. She scrapes her body on the tree's bark whenever she feels an itch. This action is significant because it shows that even the most "sacred" entity in the eyes of the people does not practice the caste-based untouchability that humans do. By highlighting that the gods residing within the cow touch the tree, the poet exposes the hypocrisy of those who claim to be religious but practice discrimination against fellow human beings.

2. Explain the significance of the title "If I was a Tree".
The title "If I was a Tree" represents the poet's deep longing for freedom and equality. A tree is a symbol of life, endurance, and connection to the earth. By wishing to be a tree, the poet expresses his desire to escape the suffocating social structure of the caste system. It is a cry for a life where one is judged by their existence rather than their birth. The title encapsulates the entire theme of the poem: the preference for a natural, non-judgmental existence over a human life marred by prejudice and social exclusion.

3. Discuss the role of nature as a silent witness in the poem.
Nature acts as a silent witness that exposes the cruelty of human society. The bird, the sunlight, the breeze, the rain, and the earth are all depicted as entities that operate on the principle of equality. They do not ask for caste, they do not feel defiled, and they do not exclude anyone. By placing these natural elements in contrast with the poet's experience of being an outcast, the poet uses nature to judge humanity. Nature becomes the standard of morality, showing that while humans have advanced in technology and religion, they have failed to achieve the basic human decency that exists in the natural world.
