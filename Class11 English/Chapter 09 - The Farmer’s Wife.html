Chapter 9: The Farmer’s Wife - Volga

English Overview

The poem "The Farmer’s Wife" by Volga is a poignant reflection on the plight of farmers in India, specifically focusing on the aftermath of a farmer's suicide. The speaker, the farmer's wife, addresses her deceased husband, questioning his decision to end his life while leaving her to face the crushing burden of debt, social stigma, and the responsibility of raising their four children. The poem highlights the gendered nature of suffering, where the woman is left to endure the "harvest of her womb" (her children) while the husband finds an "easy" escape through poison. Ultimately, the poem shifts from grief and anger to a powerful assertion of survival, as the wife decides to embrace life and fight for her children's future rather than succumbing to despair.

Poem Memory Support

1. Poem Summary

The poem is a dramatic monologue where a widow confronts her dead husband who committed suicide due to crop failure and debt. She contrasts his "virtuous" exit with her "bitter existence." She expresses anger at his cowardice in leaving her with the burden of four children. She realizes that while he escaped, she must stay and fight for her children's survival.

2. Central Idea

The central idea is the resilience of a woman who, despite being abandoned by her husband's suicide, chooses to live and struggle for her children rather than giving up.

3. Theme

The themes include the agrarian crisis, the gendered impact of farmer suicides, the burden of poverty, and the strength of the human spirit to survive against all odds.

4. Poet Message

Volga conveys that suicide is not a solution to life's problems. True courage lies in facing the struggle of life and protecting one's family, rather than choosing the "easy" path of death.

5. Easy Memorization Notes

  • Stanza 1: Husband's suicide vs. Wife's struggle.
  • Stanza 2: The burden of debt and the husband's past abuse.
  • Stanza 3: The responsibility of the four children.
  • Stanza 4: The resolution to live and fight.

6. 5 Mark Answer: The contrast between the husband and the wife.

In "The Farmer's Wife," the poet draws a sharp contrast between the husband and the wife. The husband, unable to face the burden of debt and crop failure, chooses to end his life by consuming poison. He is described as "virtuous" in a sarcastic sense, as he escapes the creditors. In contrast, the wife is left behind to face the harsh reality. She has been "born with a head bent" and "a hand outstretched," accustomed to hardship. While the husband finds a quick release in death, the wife is forced to endure a "bitter existence." She is left to manage the debt, the social shame, and the upbringing of their four children. The husband's act is portrayed as an act of cowardice, whereas the wife's decision to continue living is portrayed as an act of immense courage and strength.

7. 10 Mark Answer: The theme of survival and the struggle for life.

The poem "The Farmer's Wife" is a powerful testament to the resilience of women in the face of agrarian distress. The speaker, a widow, is initially filled with anger and resentment toward her husband for his suicide. She questions his decision to leave her with the "harvest of her womb"—their four children—while he chose the easy way out. The poem explores the deep emotional and physical toll of poverty. The wife recalls the abuse she suffered at his hands, yet she remains committed to their children. The "worm-eaten cotton pods" symbolize their ruined crops and their ruined lives. However, the poem takes a transformative turn at the end. The wife realizes that she cannot succumb to the same fate as her husband. She declares, "I must live / I must embrace life not death." This shift from despair to determination highlights the theme of survival. She decides to teach her children to "clench a fist," not just for food, but to fight the battle of life. The poem ultimately celebrates the strength of a woman who refuses to be defeated by circumstances, choosing instead to embrace the struggle for life.
