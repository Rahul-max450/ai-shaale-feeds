SECTION 1: CHAPTER OVERVIEW

English Overview

The poem "An Old Woman" by Arun Kolatkar is a poignant reflection on the encounter between a tourist and an elderly woman in the temple town of Jejuri. The poem begins with the woman persistently following the narrator, offering to guide him to a "horseshoe shrine" in exchange for a fifty-paise coin. Initially, the narrator views her as a nuisance, comparing her to a "burr" and wanting to end the "farce." However, when the woman questions what else an old woman can do in such a wretched place, the narrator's perspective shifts. He looks into her eyes and sees "bullet holes," symbolizing the harshness of her life. The poem concludes with a powerful imagery of the world cracking around her, suggesting that her existence is more enduring than the landscape itself. Ultimately, the narrator feels diminished, realizing that he is merely "small change" in the hands of this resilient, "shatterproof" woman.

SECTION 2: POEM MEMORY SUPPORT

1. Poem Summary

The poem depicts a chance encounter at Jejuri. An old woman insists on being a guide for a small fee. The narrator is annoyed by her persistence. However, when she speaks about the hopelessness of her situation, the narrator realizes the depth of her struggle. The landscape, the temples, and the sky seem to shatter, revealing the woman's indomitable spirit.

2. Central Idea

The central idea is the contrast between the insignificance of human life in the face of poverty and the immense, enduring strength of the human spirit. It highlights how a person's suffering can shatter the observer's perception of reality.

3. Theme

The themes include poverty, the resilience of the elderly, the indifference of society, and the transformation of perception through empathy.

4. Poet Message

The poet conveys that we should look beyond the surface of people, especially the marginalized, and recognize the profound struggle and strength they carry within them.

5. Easy Memorization Notes

  • Stanza 1: The encounter (The woman, the coin, the shrine).
  • Stanza 2: The annoyance (The burr, the desire to end the farce).
  • Stanza 3: The realization (The question, the bullet-hole eyes).
  • Stanza 4: The shattering (Hills, temples, sky cracking).
  • Stanza 5: The conclusion (Small change, shatterproof crone).

6. 5 Mark Answer (The transformation of the speaker)

In the poem "An Old Woman," the speaker's attitude undergoes a significant transformation. Initially, he views the old woman as a nuisance, a beggar who is pestering him for money. He describes her as a "burr" that sticks to his clothes, and he is determined to end the "farce" of her company. However, when she asks, "What else can an old woman do on hills as wretched as these?", the speaker is forced to look at her differently. He looks into her eyes and sees "bullet holes," which represent the trauma and hardship she has endured. This moment of empathy shatters his previous arrogance. He realizes that her life is defined by struggle, and compared to her resilience, his own existence feels insignificant. He is humbled, feeling reduced to "small change" in her presence, acknowledging her as a "shatterproof" figure who has survived the harshness of time and place.

7. 10 Mark Answer (Critical Analysis of the Poem)

Arun Kolatkar’s "An Old Woman" is a powerful commentary on the intersection of poverty, landscape, and human endurance. The poem is set in Jejuri, a place of religious significance, yet the atmosphere is one of decay and "wretched" hills. The old woman, who acts as a self-appointed guide, represents the marginalized population that survives on the fringes of such tourist spots. The poet uses the metaphor of a "burr" to describe her persistence, highlighting how society often views the poor as an irritation rather than human beings with stories. The turning point occurs when the narrator looks into her eyes. By describing her eyes as "bullet holes," Kolatkar suggests that she has been a victim of life's violence and neglect. The subsequent imagery of the hills, temples, and sky cracking signifies that the woman’s suffering is so profound that it threatens to dismantle the very reality of the place. The "shatterproof crone" is a symbol of survival; she has outlasted the temples and the landscape. The poem concludes with the narrator feeling reduced to "small change," a metaphor for his own insignificance when confronted with the raw, enduring reality of the woman’s existence. Through this, Kolatkar forces the reader to confront their own prejudices and recognize the dignity and strength inherent in those we often dismiss.

SECTION 4: EXTRA EXAM QUESTIONS

Question 1: Discuss the significance of the title "An Old Woman" in the context of the poem.

Answer: The title "An Old Woman" is simple yet profound. It highlights the anonymity of the woman; she is not given a name, representing the countless elderly, marginalized women who struggle for survival in tourist destinations. By calling her just "an old woman," the poet emphasizes that she is a common sight, often ignored or treated as a nuisance by passersby. However, the poem reveals that this "ordinary" woman possesses an extraordinary, "shatterproof" spirit. The title serves as a contrast between the societal perception of her as insignificant and the reality of her immense resilience. It forces the reader to look past the label and see the human being behind it. The title effectively sets the stage for the transformation of the narrator's perspective from indifference to deep realization.

Question 2: How does the poet use the landscape of Jejuri to reflect the woman's condition?

Answer: The poet uses the landscape of Jejuri—its hills, temples, and sky—to mirror the old woman's internal and external state. The hills are described as "wretched," reflecting the poverty and hopelessness of the environment in which she lives. The temples, usually symbols of faith and stability, are shown as "cracking," suggesting that the religious and social structures of the place are decaying. When the woman’s eyes are described as "bullet holes," it links her personal trauma to the harshness of the landscape. The ultimate "shattering" of the hills, temples, and sky signifies that the woman’s suffering is so intense that it transcends her individual self and affects the entire environment. The landscape is not just a backdrop; it is an extension of her struggle, making her survival even more remarkable.

Question 3: Analyze the role of the "fifty paise coin" in the poem.

Answer: The fifty-paise coin is the catalyst for the entire encounter. It represents the transactional nature of the relationship between the tourist and the local. For the narrator, it is a small amount of money he is willing to pay to get rid of the "burr." For the old woman, it is a means of survival, a way to earn her keep in a place that offers no other opportunities. The coin symbolizes the gap between the two worlds: the tourist who views the place as a destination and the woman who views it as a site of struggle. When the narrator feels reduced to "small change," the coin takes on a deeper meaning. It signifies that in the face of the woman's enduring spirit, the narrator's material wealth is trivial. The coin is no longer just money; it is a symbol of the narrator's own insignificance and the woman's ultimate power.
