Chapter Overview: It Never Comes Again

English Overview: The poem "It Never Comes Again" by Richard Henry Stoddard explores the fleeting nature of youth. The poet acknowledges that while life offers compensations for many material losses, the departure of youth is an irreversible loss that leaves a permanent void in the human heart. It emphasizes that youth is the most precious phase of life, filled with dreams and vitality, and once it passes, it can never be reclaimed.

Kannada Translation: ರಿಚರ್ಡ್ ಹೆನ್ರಿ ಸ್ಟೊಡಾರ್ಡ್ ಅವರ "ಇಟ್ ನೆವರ್ ಕಮ್ಸ್ ಅಗೇನ್" ಕವಿತೆಯು ಯೌವನದ ಕ್ಷಣಿಕ ಸ್ವಭಾವವನ್ನು ಪರಿಶೋಧಿಸುತ್ತದೆ. ಜೀವನದಲ್ಲಿ ಭೌತಿಕ ನಷ್ಟಗಳಿಗೆ ಪರಿಹಾರಗಳಿದ್ದರೂ, ಯೌವನದ ನಿರ್ಗಮನವು ಮನುಷ್ಯನ ಹೃದಯದಲ್ಲಿ ಶಾಶ್ವತವಾದ ಶೂನ್ಯವನ್ನು ಉಂಟುಮಾಡುವ ಬದಲಾಯಿಸಲಾಗದ ನಷ್ಟವಾಗಿದೆ ಎಂದು ಕವಿ ಒಪ್ಪಿಕೊಳ್ಳುತ್ತಾರೆ. ಯೌವನವು ಕನಸುಗಳು ಮತ್ತು ಚೈತನ್ಯದಿಂದ ತುಂಬಿದ ಜೀವನದ ಅತ್ಯಂತ ಅಮೂಲ್ಯವಾದ ಹಂತವಾಗಿದೆ ಮತ್ತು ಅದು ಒಮ್ಮೆ ಕಳೆದರೆ ಮತ್ತೆಂದೂ ಮರಳಿ ಬರುವುದಿಲ್ಲ ಎಂದು ಇದು ಒತ್ತಿಹೇಳುತ್ತದೆ.

Poem Memory Support

1. Poem Summary: The poem reflects on the transient nature of youth. The poet suggests that while we can recover from material losses or find comfort for our pains, the loss of youth is unique. As we grow older, we may become stronger and more mature, but we always miss the sweetness and dreams associated with our younger days. This beauty vanishes, and despite our longing, it never returns.

2. Central Idea: The central idea is the irreversibility of time and the preciousness of youth. It highlights that youth is a "dream" that defines our early life, and its departure is a profound loss that cannot be replaced by the maturity of adulthood.

3. Theme: The themes include the passage of time, the inevitability of aging, the value of youth, and the human tendency to look back with nostalgia at lost phases of life.

4. Poet Message: The poet conveys that since youth is a fleeting phase that never returns, one should cherish it and make the best use of it while it lasts.

5. Easy Memorization Notes:

  • Stanza 1: Gains for losses, balms for pain, but youth (the dream) departs and never returns.
  • Stanza 2: We become stronger in manhood, but we miss the "sweet" feeling of youth that fled away.
  • Stanza 3: Beauty vanishes, we sigh in vain, we see it everywhere but it is gone forever.

6. 5 Mark Answer (Theme/Message): The poem "It Never Comes Again" serves as a poignant reminder of the fleeting nature of youth. The poet Richard Henry Stoddard beautifully contrasts the losses we face in life with the loss of youth. He notes that for most pains and losses, there are "balms" or remedies, but the departure of youth is absolute. Youth is described as a "dream" that fills our hearts with hope and sweetness. Even though adulthood brings strength and stability, it lacks the magical, dream-like quality of youth. The poet emphasizes that we often look back at our youth with longing, but it is a phase that, once gone, is lost forever. This teaches us the importance of valuing our time and living our youth to the fullest, as it is a once-in-a-lifetime experience.

7. 10 Mark Answer (Critical Appreciation): Richard Henry Stoddard’s poem "It Never Comes Again" is a reflective piece on the inevitable passage of time. The poem is structured into three stanzas, each highlighting a different aspect of aging. In the first stanza, the poet establishes that while life offers compensations for material losses, the loss of youth is irreplaceable. He calls youth a "dream," suggesting it is an idealistic and beautiful time. In the second stanza, he acknowledges that adulthood brings "sterner" strength, yet there is a lingering sadness for the "sweet" things that left with our youth. The third stanza captures the universal human experience of nostalgia. We "sigh in vain" because we see the remnants of youth everywhere—in the earth and air—but we cannot physically reclaim that time. The poet uses personification, such as youth having "flying feet," to show how quickly it escapes us. The poem serves as a philosophical meditation on the cycle of life. It encourages readers to appreciate the present moment. By the end, the reader understands that youth is not just a biological phase but a state of mind and heart that defines our capacity to dream. The poem’s simple yet profound language makes it a timeless classic about the human condition.

1. Long Answer: Why does the poet call youth a 'dream'? Explain the significance of this metaphor.

English Answer: The poet calls youth a 'dream' because, like a dream, youth is idealistic, beautiful, and fleeting. Dreams often represent our deepest desires and hopes, which are most vibrant during our younger years. By using this metaphor, the poet suggests that youth is not just a physical age but a state of mind characterized by imagination and endless possibilities. Just as a dream fades upon waking, youth fades as we enter the "sterner reign" of adulthood. This metaphor emphasizes the transient and ethereal nature of this life phase, highlighting that while it is incredibly sweet and precious, it lacks the permanence of reality, making its departure inevitable and deeply felt.

2. Long Answer: Discuss the contrast between the 'gains' of adulthood and the 'loss' of youth as presented in the poem.

English Answer: The poem presents a clear contrast between the gains of adulthood and the loss of youth. As we grow older, we gain strength, maturity, and stability, which the poet refers to as "manhood’s sterner reign." This is a positive gain in terms of character and capability. However, this gain comes at a cost. We lose the "sweetness" and the dream-like quality of youth. The poet suggests that while we become better and stronger, we are left with a sense of emptiness because something beautiful has vanished. We try to find that lost sweetness everywhere, but it is gone. Thus, the poem argues that while adulthood has its own merits, it can never fully compensate for the loss of the innocence and vitality that defined our youth.

3. Long Answer: How does the poet use the imagery of 'flying feet' to describe the nature of youth?

English Answer: The poet uses the imagery of 'flying feet' to describe youth as something that moves very quickly and is difficult to hold onto. By attributing 'flying feet' to youth, the poet personifies it, suggesting that youth is like a person who is constantly running away from us. This imagery effectively captures the fleeting nature of this phase. No matter how much we want to keep our youth, it moves forward with speed, leaving us behind in the process of aging. This creates a sense of urgency and nostalgia, as the reader realizes that time is constantly slipping away. The 'flying feet' metaphor serves as a powerful reminder that youth is a temporary visitor that does not stay long, and once it has run its course, it never returns to us.
