Class: 9th English • CHAPTER 14 - NOBLENESS ENKINDLETH NOBLENESS

Chapter Overview: Nobleness Enkindleth Nobleness

English Overview: The poem "Nobleness Enkindleth Nobleness" by James Russell Lowell tells the story of Yussouf, a man known for his kindness. A stranger, who is an outcast and in fear, seeks shelter in Yussouf's tent. Yussouf welcomes him, believing that his home belongs to God. The next morning, Yussouf provides the stranger with a horse and gold to help him escape. Overwhelmed by this generosity, the stranger confesses that he is the one who killed Yussouf's son. Instead of seeking revenge, Yussouf gives him more gold and tells him to leave, feeling that his son's death has been avenged through this act of forgiveness. The poem highlights that kindness and nobility inspire more of the same in others.

Poem Memory Support

1. Poem Summary: The poem depicts a profound act of forgiveness. Yussouf, a noble man, shelters a stranger in his tent. Despite the stranger being the murderer of his son, Yussouf treats him with extreme kindness, providing him with gold and a horse to escape his pursuers. This act of grace transforms the stranger, leading him to confess his crime. Yussouf’s response—offering more gold and letting him go—demonstrates that true nobility has the power to change hearts.

2. Central Idea: The central idea is that "nobleness enkindleth nobleness." It suggests that kindness is contagious. When we treat others with compassion, even those who have wronged us, we inspire them to reflect on their actions and potentially change their ways.

3. Theme: The themes include forgiveness, generosity, the transient nature of worldly possessions, and the transformative power of love and mercy over hatred and revenge.

4. Poet Message: James Russell Lowell conveys that we should not hold onto grudges. By practicing forgiveness and treating everyone as a child of God, we can overcome the cycle of violence and bring peace to our own hearts and the world.

5. Easy Memorization Notes:

  • Stanza 1: The stranger arrives at Yussouf's tent in fear.
  • Stanza 2: Yussouf welcomes him, saying the tent belongs to God.
  • Stanza 3: Yussouf gives him gold and a horse to escape.
  • Stanza 4: The stranger confesses he killed Yussouf's son.
  • Stanza 5: Yussouf forgives him, gives more gold, and finds peace.

6. 5 Mark Answer (The Character of Yussouf): Yussouf is portrayed as a man of immense character and spiritual depth. He does not view his possessions as his own but as gifts from God, which makes him incredibly generous. When a stranger comes to him in fear, he does not ask questions but offers immediate shelter. His nobility is tested when he discovers the stranger is his son's killer. Instead of succumbing to anger, he chooses to forgive. This shows that Yussouf values human life and moral integrity above personal vengeance. His actions prove that he is truly "The Good," as he believes that by showing mercy, he is fulfilling God's will. His ability to let go of his "black thought" (the desire for revenge) brings him inner peace and sets a powerful example of how to live a virtuous life.

7. 10 Mark Answer (The Message of the Poem): The poem "Nobleness Enkindleth Nobleness" serves as a moral guide for humanity. The title itself is the core message: noble actions inspire noble responses. Yussouf’s behavior towards the stranger is a perfect example of this. By treating the stranger with kindness, Yussouf forces the stranger to confront his own guilt. The stranger, who came in fear, leaves with a transformed heart because he experienced unconditional mercy. The poem teaches us that revenge only perpetuates pain, whereas forgiveness heals. Yussouf’s perspective on God’s ownership of all things removes the ego from his actions, allowing him to be selfless. Furthermore, the poem emphasizes that we are all connected under God’s "glorious roof." By choosing to forgive the man who killed his first-born, Yussouf achieves a higher level of spiritual maturity. He realizes that his son's death cannot be undone by killing another, but it can be balanced by an act of supreme goodness. Ultimately, the poet encourages readers to adopt this spirit of generosity and forgiveness to create a more harmonious world.

Vocabulary Task

Question: Find the meaning of these words: Pity, forgive, condole, console, grant, amnesty, compassion, mercy, lament, pardon, reprieve, solace, asylum.

Answer:

  • Pity: Feeling of sorrow for someone's misfortune.
  • Forgive: To stop feeling angry toward someone who has offended you.
  • Condole: To express sympathy for someone who is grieving.
  • Console: To comfort someone in a time of grief.
  • Grant: To agree to give or allow something.
  • Amnesty: An official pardon for people who have been convicted of political offenses.
  • Compassion: Sympathetic pity and concern for the sufferings of others.
  • Mercy: Compassion or forgiveness shown toward someone whom it is within one's power to punish.
  • Lament: A passionate expression of grief or sorrow.
  • Pardon: The action of forgiving or being forgiven for an error or offense.
  • Reprieve: A cancellation or postponement of a punishment.
  • Solace: Comfort or consolation in a time of distress.
  • Asylum: The protection granted by a nation to someone who has left their native country as a political refugee.

Understand the Poem

Question 1: Who came to Yussouf’s tent one night?
Answer: A stranger who was an outcast and in fear came to Yussouf’s tent.

Question 2: What did he say?
Answer: He said that he was an outcast, in fear of his life, and needed shelter and food.

Question 3: Did he go to the tent for food and shelter?
Answer: Yes, he went to the tent specifically seeking shelter and food.

Question 4: Was Yussouf called ‘The Good’?
Answer: Yes, Yussouf was known through all the tribes as "The Good."

Question 5: Was Yussouf kind to the stranger?
Answer: Yes, Yussouf was extremely kind; he provided the stranger with shelter, food, a horse, and gold.

Question 6: Did Yussouf believe in God?
Answer: Yes, Yussouf believed that his tent and all his possessions belonged to God.

Question 7: What did Yussouf say, waking up the stranger?
Answer: He told the stranger to take his gold and his swiftest horse and depart before the day grew bold.

Question 8: What made the stranger’s face grand?
Answer: The "inward light" that shines from self-conquest made the stranger's face grand.

Question 9: Who killed Yussouf’s son?
Answer: The stranger, Ibrahim, killed Yussouf’s son.

Question 10: Did Yussouf get angry?
Answer: No, Yussouf did not get angry; instead, he offered the stranger more gold.

Question 11: What did Yussouf say to Ibrahim?
Answer: Yussouf told him to take thrice the gold and leave, saying that his own "black thought" (desire for revenge) would ride away with him into the desert.

Question 12: Who did Yussouf yearn for day and night?
Answer: Yussouf yearned for his first-born son.

Read and Appreciate

Question 1: How has the poet described the stranger?
Answer: The poet describes the stranger as an "outcast" who is in "dread," fleeing from the "bow of power" (enemies) and having nowhere to lay his head.

Question 2: Pick out the lines which show Yussouf’s respect for God.
Answer: "The tent is mine," said Yussouf, "but no more / Than it is God’s; come in, and be at peace."

Question 3: Explain: “As one lamp lights another, nor grows less, So nobleness enkindleth nobleness.”
Answer: This means that just as a candle can light another without losing its own flame, an act of kindness inspires others to be kind, without diminishing the original person's goodness.

Question 4: Which line gives the idea that God is impartial?
Answer: "Balanced and just are all of God’s decrees."

Question 5: What message does the poet give us through this poem?
Answer: The poet teaches us that forgiveness and generosity are the highest forms of nobility and that they have the power to transform even the most hardened hearts.

Question 6: Which line do you like the most and why?
Answer: "So nobleness enkindleth nobleness" is the most impactful line because it summarizes the entire philosophy of the poem—that goodness is a self-sustaining and spreading force.

Extra Exam Questions

1. Why did Yussouf offer the stranger more gold after knowing he was his son's killer?
Yussouf offered more gold because he wanted to release himself from the burden of hatred. He realized that holding onto the desire for revenge was a "black thought" that haunted him day and night. By showing extreme generosity to the man who caused him the greatest pain, Yussouf achieved a state of spiritual victory. He believed that God’s decrees are balanced and just, and by forgiving the murderer, he was finally able to find peace for his son's soul and his own heart.

2. Discuss the significance of the title "Nobleness Enkindleth Nobleness."
The title is the central theme of the poem. It suggests that nobility is not a static quality but an active, infectious one. When Yussouf acts with nobility, he "enkindles" or ignites a similar spark in the stranger. The stranger, who was initially an outcast in fear, is moved to confess his crime and show remorse. This proves that kindness is a powerful tool for social and personal reform. The title serves as a reminder that our actions have the potential to bring out the best in others.

3. How does the poem reflect the poet's belief in the link between poetry and social reform?
James Russell Lowell believed that poetry should serve a higher purpose, such as social reform. In this poem, he uses the narrative of Yussouf to advocate for the values of forgiveness and mercy over the cycle of violence. By presenting a story where a murderer is redeemed through an act of grace, Lowell challenges the reader to reconsider their own attitudes toward enemies and social outcasts. The poem acts as a moral lesson, encouraging readers to adopt a more compassionate worldview, which is a form of social reform starting from the individual heart.
