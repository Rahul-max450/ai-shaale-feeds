THE FIRST WOMAN IN SPACE - Valentina Tereshkova

English Overview

Chapter 19, "The First Woman in Space," recounts the inspiring saga of Valentina Tereshkova, a Soviet cosmonaut who made history on June 16, 1963, by becoming the first woman to travel into space aboard the Vostok VI spacecraft. Born into a humble family in Maselennikovo in 1937, Valentina, affectionately known as Valya, faced significant hardships early in life, including the loss of her father during World War II and family poverty. These circumstances prevented her from having continuous schooling, leading her to work at a tyre factory and then a textile mill from the age of seventeen to support her mother. Despite these challenges, she pursued education by attending classes at Yaroslav and graduating from a trade school in 1960.

A pivotal moment in her life was her training in parachute jumping at the Yaroslav Aero Club, where her boldness and daring stood out. Inspired by Yuri Gagarin's pioneering space flight, she secretly harbored a dream of flying into space herself. Her determination led her to apply and be admitted to a space school, paving the way for her extraordinary journey. During her rigorous cosmonaut training, Valentina's keen interest and persistent efforts allowed her to excel, surpassing her fellow trainees and earning her selection for the space mission.

Her three-day mission in space was a global event, met with immense joy and pride. From an altitude of 113 to 144 miles, she communicated with millions on Earth, describing her thrilling experience. She observed the cloud-covered Earth and recognized landscapes like the River Volga. Despite traveling at an unimaginable speed, she felt normal, ate, slept, and even sang. Her flight demonstrated a woman's stamina and endurance, as she diligently made observations, took notes, and sent news back to Earth. Upon her return, she skillfully steered her ship and landed safely by parachute in Soviet Central Asia, sustaining only a minor nose injury. Her famous quote upon landing, "it is very nice to be a guest but it is always better at home," encapsulated her joy of returning.

Valentina's flight was a crucial experiment to test women's ability to withstand the rigors of rocket flight and weightlessness. Beyond her physical strength and fitness, she possessed a love for music, literature, reading, and a quiet home life. Her achievement is celebrated as a triumph of the human spirit, a source of pride for all mankind, and a significant milestone in the history of civilization.
